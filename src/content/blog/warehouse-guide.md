## Unique warehouse challenges

Warehouses present a unique blend of safety and security challenges. Heavy machinery operating alongside pedestrians, high-value inventory spread across vast floor areas, multiple access points, and round-the-clock shift patterns create a complex monitoring environment that traditional security approaches struggle to cover.

The scale of modern warehouse operations — some exceeding 100,000 square feet — makes comprehensive human monitoring practically impossible. AI-powered monitoring offers a scalable solution that grows with your operation.

## Loading bay safety

Loading bays are consistently among the most hazardous areas in warehouse operations. The combination of heavy vehicles, manual handling, and time pressure creates conditions where accidents are alarmingly common. AI monitoring adds a crucial safety layer by detecting dangerous situations before they escalate.

### Pedestrian-vehicle separation

Configure virtual zones that define pedestrian-only and vehicle-only areas within and around loading bays. When a person is detected in a vehicle zone (or vice versa), an immediate alert fires. This simple rule has prevented countless near-miss incidents at SmartGuard-equipped sites.

For added safety, combine with occupancy rules: if more than a threshold number of people are detected in a bay area during active loading, the system can alert the bay supervisor to pause operations until the area is cleared.

## After-hours security

Warehouse theft often occurs outside business hours, when skeleton security staff are responsible for monitoring large, dimly-lit spaces. Time-based rules transform your existing cameras into a comprehensive after-hours security system.

Configure critical zones — entrance points, high-value storage areas, loading docks — to switch to high-alert mode outside shift hours. Any person detected in these zones triggers an immediate escalation to security management, complete with a snapshot from the triggering camera.

## Inventory protection

Beyond traditional theft prevention, AI monitoring helps identify inventory shrinkage patterns. By tracking movement patterns in and around high-value storage areas, operations teams can identify unusual access patterns that might indicate pilferage or process failures.

Zone-based dwell-time rules can detect when someone spends an unusual amount of time in a specific area. While there may be legitimate reasons, consistent flagging of unusual patterns helps identify potential issues early.

## Deployment checklist

Start your warehouse AI monitoring deployment with this checklist: (1) Audit existing camera positions and identify coverage gaps, especially in loading bays, entry/exit points, and high-value storage. (2) Define zone maps for each camera view, starting with the five essential patterns. (3) Configure time-based rules that align with shift schedules. (4) Set up alert channels for different severity levels. (5) Run a two-week observation period before enforcing automated responses. (6) Review analytics weekly to refine zones and rules based on actual operational patterns.
